Girlfriend File

Profile File

A private file kept away from public lights. This page is for profile content.

NameNicole / Nikki / Ying AccessPrivate Archive RoleFirst Audience StatusAfter Midnight Only

By the time I opened the backend of the webpage, it was already past two in the morning.

The screen came alive. The title bar was empty, and the cursor blinked inside that little white box. I thought, at first, that it would be easy. Girlfriend profile. Sounds simple enough. Name, nicknames, things she likes, things she doesn’t. A few photographs. That should be enough to make a page look complete.

I typed Nicole.

Paused for a while.

Then I typed Nikki.

And finally, beside them, I typed ying.

Three names, sitting there in a row. I looked at them for a long time. I meant to keep going, but my hand had already reached toward the space beside the ashtray.

The lighter wasn’t there.

The desk was a beautiful little disaster. Old playbills, record sleeves, typing paper, a guitar pick, half a cup of cold coffee, and a few loose sheets I’d dropped there without thinking. While looking for the lighter, I found a photograph of a little dog first.

On the back of it, she had written one sentence:

There are no gaps between the paws. Only fluff.

I stared at that line for a few seconds and laughed.

When she sent me that photograph, she had been terribly serious about it. I told her it sounded like some kind of medical conclusion. She replied almost at once:

You don’t understand.

And she was right. I didn’t. Not really.

That fluffy little creature looked straight into the camera with a very grave expression, as if it were deciding whether I had any right to exist near its house. I said maybe it didn’t like me.

She answered:

It’s good at judging people.

A few minutes later, she sent another photograph. The dog was lying beside her, half its face buried in fur. The picture was a little blurry, the light nothing special. Her hand appeared at the edge of the frame, fingers resting on the dog’s back.

I looked at that hand, and suddenly I could almost hear the way she spoke.

And then I thought, well, it didn’t matter much whether that fluffy little creature liked me or not.

My own fluffy little creature liked me.

The lighter still hadn’t turned up.

I kept searching and found a ring box. Empty now. The ring was with her.

I found it in an old shop. Silver, worn, with markings that looked as if they had already survived a few lives. It didn’t shine very much behind the glass. The shopkeeper told me it had been sitting there for a long time, and hardly anyone ever asked about it. I asked him to take it out. Held it in my hand for a while.

By then, I was already thinking of Nikki.

I knew she wouldn’t like something too new. Anything too bright, too clean, too eager to be loved would look awkward in her hands. When she looks at old things, she takes her time. Her fingers touch the edges first, then she turns the thing over, as if waiting for it to explain where it came from.

When I gave her the ring, she didn’t put it on right away.

She asked, “Who did this belong to before?”

I said, “No idea. Maybe someone with a rotten temper. Maybe some unlucky poet. Maybe just someone who bought a ring and regretted it later.”

She lowered her head and smiled.

“You make things up very smoothly.”

“I respect every old object’s right to talk nonsense.”

She held the ring in her palm for a while. Then she put it on and raised her hand toward the light.

Once it was on her hand, the ring suddenly had a place.

She didn’t say whether she liked it. She only turned her wrist a little and watched the light slip across the silver.

Later, when we went out to eat, she was still wearing it. The menu lay open on the table. She lowered her head to read the names of the dishes, and her other hand, under the table, lightly touched the surface of the ring.

Writing this down, I remembered the night she sat in my room watching a film.

The room was dark. The screen was glowing. She sat on the rug, her drink beside her knee. She doesn’t talk much in the middle of a movie. Even when the film is long, she waits until a part has truly ended before putting her cup down.

That night, she asked me, “After you finish playing a character, do you miss him?”

That question kept me quiet for a while.

People usually ask whether a role was fun. Whether the costume was difficult. Which scene was the hardest.

I said yes.

Some of them, yes.

She didn’t chase the answer. Didn’t put on that expression people wear when they’re waiting for a story to be performed for them. She only nodded and kept looking at the screen.

After a while, she said, “Then he probably remembers you too.”

I looked at her.

She was still watching the film, as if what she had just said were only a coin she had casually placed on the table.

I wrote that sentence down later.

When she watches my work, she rarely tears me and the character apart in some rough, careless way. She knows that when I play a person, I really do go close to him. She also knows that once you’ve gone close, you don’t walk back out completely clean.

She doesn’t treat those characters like noisy little labels. She doesn’t treat me like a face people have grown used to looking at.

Her questions always land somewhere further in.

On the webpage, I wrote:

She is my first audience.

It looked a little solemn. I stared at it for a few seconds, but I didn’t delete it.

Because it was true.

Then I found a photograph by the sea.

A blue dress. A straw hat. A strong wind. One of her hands held the brim of the hat down, and the other reached toward the camera, as if the person taking the picture had been standing too far away. There was a heart drawn in the sand. Not perfectly round. The edges had been thinned by wind and sand.

She was smiling in the photograph. Her hair was a mess. The hem of her dress was a mess too.

I’ve looked at that picture many times.

She looks bright in it. But that brightness is not empty. She likes blue. She likes the sea. She also likes mysteries, horror films, old movies, and stories with shadows in them.

She can laugh lightly by the sea, and she can also ask, quite seriously, whether a character might remember the actor who once played him.

She looks after little dogs. She saves a place for an old playbill. She also keeps time, distance, money, and tomorrow’s tasks all somewhere in her mind, quietly calculating them.

Before she moves close to anything, she thinks it through.

That isn’t hesitation.

She simply knows that to like something, to love someone, costs real things. Time. Energy. Heart. Distance. And many things that can’t be written on a receipt.

After she has counted all of that and still walks forward, that step carries weight.

I put the seaside photograph into the webpage folder and renamed the file blue.

Then I thought it was too simple.

Changed it to nikki_blue.

Still simple.

In the end, I didn’t change it again.

The lover profile on the screen filled up little by little. Not in the way I first imagined. Not like a form. Not like a few pretty lines of introduction.

It began turning into things I actually remembered.

I finally found the lighter in the pocket of my coat.

I stood by the desk and looked for a while at everything I had pulled out. The little dog photograph was slipped back into the playbill. The ring box hadn’t been closed properly. The seaside photograph was still waiting on the right side of the screen.

And the webpage title was no longer empty.

I had meant to tidy the desk.

Put everything back where it belonged. Make the page look more like a proper gift.

In the end, I didn’t.

She had been here once. I had found the lighter. And I didn’t much feel like putting the room back the way it was.

Memo / Typewriter

Typed Memo

The typewriter keeps the little things: habits, distance, and private notes.

Time Difference

Shanghai stayed pinned to the first screen, Los Angeles beside it. The numbers looked harmless enough, until her night fell into my daylight. Only then did I realize the distance between us was far too great.

I began to miss her.

Her good night often arrived while someone was still knocking on my door.

My good morning sometimes reached her when her room had already gone quiet.

This planet is terribly rude.

Messages

When she is outside, her replies are short.

After she gets back to her room, they grow a little longer.

Sometimes it is only a small proof.

Proof that, when she stopped somewhere in the world, she thought of me.

Our Movie List

Horror.

Suspense.

Strange old things.

Nothing too polished.

Notes beside the titles:

Bad ending. / Good silence. / Pause here at 01:17:32. / Watch together. / She will hate this. / She might like this.

Nail Polish

The nail polish she put on me.

The blue came off first.

The red stayed a little longer.

A bit of metallic color survived on the edge of one nail through three interviews.

When someone asked about it, I put my hand in my pocket and thought of her lowering her head, holding my fingers, taking every color seriously.

Language

Translation software always does terrible things to her Chinese.

There was one sentence it turned into something like a police report written by a depressed machine.

Later, she explained it to me herself.

The same words suddenly had warmth.

Nikki’s mouth seemed to have some kind of magic in it.

Sticky Notes

The sticky notes sent from Shanghai were tucked inside a book.

Her handwriting sat in the margins.

One word had been circled twice.

A question mark followed a line of poetry.

The blue ink pressed harder near the end, as if that sentence had offended her.
